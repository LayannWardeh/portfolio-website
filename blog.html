Programming Languages & Innovation

I found this week's readings very interesting, especially when it comes to the language a programmer uses. A lot of the emphasis was on how the programming language a programmer uses dictates the way they think and the way they solve problems. After the reading, that constraint became obvious to me, especially with the example of a programmer only using the language "Basic" and thus not knowing recursion.

However, as I reflect on my experiences with programming languages, I find it difficult to find a language that I'd use that is unique to the work I would like to do. In high school, I wanted to take another AP class (to have a better-looking transcript), and the only AP that fit in my schedule was AP Computer Science A. Though that class had a prerequisite, so I had to take an online summer course to fulfill that requirement. The online class was taught in Python. Thus, the first ever language I learned was Python. Then, during the AP class, Java was taught. Finally, during my senior year, I took another programming class, which was taught in Java and we learned a bit of SQL and HTML.

What I am trying to say is that during all of these courses, I was never given a chance to pick a language, rather I was given one to learn. But in addition to that, I was never taught the differences between these languages and how each language is more useful in different areas (till university at least). With that being said, I think it is similar to how babies are raisedâ€¦ You don't pick the language you learn as a kid, but that's what you learn, and what you become good at.

Connecting back to the discussion we had in class about learning being more tailored to the individual, I wonder if there could be more opportunities (outside of this class) for students to learn a language of their choice, but with the context of these readings. If I was given the choice to learn one programming language prior to this discussion and at the very beginning of my programming journey, I would have chosen Python because of its ease of use and widespread resources. However, I wonder if providing this context to me early on would have changed my choice or if it would have encouraged me to look into the different strengths and uses of not widely used programming languages.

Another interesting idea in the readings was about how businesses only excel when they are very much out of the ordinary. Interestingly, tying in how using an unconventional, powerful language is a way to "be doing something odd" that yields superior results. At first, I wanted to challenge this idea and argue that the language a programmer uses doesn't really affect the outcome. However, I thought about literature, specifically the Arabic language. In my (very humble) opinion, one of the most beautiful languages to read and one of the most descriptive languages is the Arabic language. As someone who grew up learning three languages, Arabic, English, and French, I always found that what I read in Arabic was a lot more comprehensive and rich. With this in mind, I want to go back to the idea about certain programming languages can be a driving factor in superior results and say that, yes I do agree with that. If on the literature level, different languages can have different results, then why isn't aren't different programming languages' results perceived in the same way?

For this coming project, I hope I can apply the value I learned from this reading about the choice behind programming language my group chooses.

Reading 03

The Sierras: The Eighties

ðŸ“… September 14, 2025 746 words Hackers Part Three
Click to read â–¼

Reading through Part Three of Hackers this week, the "Game Hackers" section, really got me thinking. What struck me most was watching hacking evolve from this pure love of discovery into something tangled up with money, fame, and business ambitions. Levy describes these new hackers as people "who dreamed not only of the ultimate hack, but of fame, and big royalty checks."

Honestly, I can't say I was surprised by this shift. Once computers broke out of labs and garages and became something regular people could actually buy, it was pretty inevitable that someone would start thinking about markets and profit margins. But it does make you wonder, what happens to that original Hacker Ethic when the driving force isn't just curiosity anymore, but cold hard cash?

When I compare these game hackers to the earlier generations, there are definitely similarities. They still had that obsessive love for computing, still pulled all-nighters perfecting their code, still got creative with solutions. But here's the key difference: their relationship with the marketplace was completely different. The earlier hackers fought against institutions and rules (and stayed away from women lol), while these Game Hackers started embracing those same systems, in some ways. Levy quotes Ken Williams as being "convinced that hackers should be stifled; what's needed is to guess what the marketplace wants, access to distribution channels, money, gimmicks, market promotion." That's so far away from Peter Samson wandering MIT's halls at 2 AM just for the pure joy of messing around with computers. It really shows how chasing commercial success can shift your priorities, even when you started with that genuine hacker spirit.

There's this question Levy poses that really stuck with me: "Did you really benefit from your computer if you did not program it?" For the Game Hackers, this seemed way less important than it was for earlier generations. By the 1980s, plenty of people were getting real value from their computers without being programmers, they had games and apps that others created for them. But I think Levy's onto something here. Without being able to create or tinker with your tools, you're missing out on some of the magic that makes computing special. He also asks whether it's good for the public when one company owns a piece of software. This is where things get really interesting for me. Sure, proprietary software created stability, jobs, and gave companies reasons to keep innovating. But it also threw up walls around the things hackers cared most about: sharing knowledge, freedom to experiment, and collaboration.

My takeaway? The Hacker Ethic can survive when money enters the picture, but it's going to change. While the whole ethic might not remain the same, we can definitely hold onto pieces of that spirit.

For me, the sweet spot is being a professional programmer who still genuinely loves computing (probably not me being this professional, but we are talking about ideas here). You can pay your bills and still make room for that hacker perfectionism. This connects back to something we've talked about in class, whether hackers have to be full-time hackersâ€¦ I really don't think so. Hackers can exist everywhere: in research labs, in politics, in game companies. What matters isn't whether they're getting paid for it, but whether they're keeping that curiosity and drive alive.

So when I think about these Game Hackers, I don't see them as completely abandoning the Hacker Ethic. They were adapting it to work in a world where hacking had to pay the bills. Whether that compromise was worth it probably depends on what you value more: staying true to ideals or actually making an impact in the world. As for me? I think you can live somewhere in between. You can participate in the commercial world while still approaching your work with a hacker's mindset.

For this week's blog, I'd like to reflect on Part 2 of Hackers: Heroes of the Computer Revolution. As I read (listenedâ€¦ audible is great because there is so much to read) through these chapters, I came to the realization that "True Hackers" and the "Hardware Hackers" are not so different in their motivations, even though their approaches and communities diverged. Both groups shared a deep belief in the hands-on imperative.

And this is apparent when the author wrote, "His goal was to break computers out of the protected AI towers, up from the depths of the dungeons of corporate accounting departments, and let people discover themselves by the Hands-On Imperative." What connected "True Hackers" and "Hardware Hackers" was their raw desire to explore, build, and create, while doing so for the purpose of hacking and joy of discovery rather than money or recognition. However, the difference came in how they wanted to hack. "True Hackers" wanted to stay in their labs hacking, while "Hardware Hackers" aimed to make computing accessible to everyone.

Can You Be a Part-Time Hacker?

In our class discussion, Professor Bui asked whether being a hacker means being a full-time hacker. To me, the answer is no. I don't think being a hacker requires abandoning every other pursuit in life. As the book shows, even someone like Lee had to balance passions with reality. For me, hacking can be a "part-time" job, something done alongside responsibilities.

In reality, everyone needs to work to provide for their needs (at least I do), and hacking doesn't always have to replace that, but it can be done alongside providing for yourself. As I start watching Mr. Robot (2 weeks and still only on episode one, but progress is being made), that's exactly what the main character does: he works his day job and becomes a hacker at night. To me, this doesn't diminish his identity as a hacker, but rather reinforces that hacking can take many forms and fit into different lifestyles.

The Free vs. Paid Software Debate

Another theme that stood out was the debate about software: should it be free or paid? Fred Moore, for instance, believed in computers as tools for community, "not only for the pleasure they gave to devoted programmers, but also for their ability to bring people together." This vision of openness influenced the idea that software should be shared, not locked away.

But others, like Bill Gates, rejected this notion, arguing that "software represented work, just as hardware did, and Altair BASIC was listed in the MITS catalog like anything else it sold." I think both sides have true-ness to them and can be solid arguments. However, as someone who always looks for and believes in a balance, I believe software should be both free and paid.

Finding Balance: The Freemium Model

Free versions open doors for exploration and learning, mirroring the Hacker Ethic of sharing knowledge, while paid versions, with premium features, recognize the labor and creativity of developers. And from a "business" standpoint (since my brain often wonders if I should've been a Mebroza), having a freemium version can open doors to customers to pay for the premium version since most people don't like to waste money on something if they don't know it is worth it or is what they want.

However, it is important to note that some programmers create just for the joy of creating, as the book puts it, "out of sheer delight in the activity for its own sake," like my friend H, who was mentioned in the last blog. These two ideas, creating for joy and creating for pay, I don't think contradict each other because each person will create based on their needs and interest/inspiration.
